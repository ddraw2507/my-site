Italy, Summer 2025: Lost in the Dolomites and Found at Lake Garda

It took me a long time to sit down and write about this trip. Maybe because the memories were too close, too full — the kind that need distance before they'll hold still long enough to become words. So here it is, months later: my account of ten days across Northern Italy in the summer of 2025.

I'll skip the two weeks of work that came before — meetings in Ferrara and Avellino, the familiar rhythm of office hours in a foreign country. This is about what happened after. This is about the Dolomites, and the people I met along the way, and the feeling of standing very small beneath very large mountains.

Day 1 — Ferrara to Bolzano: The North Begins

A light breakfast at the hotel. Check-out at 11:00. Then: four hours on the train from Ferrara to Bolzano, changing at Poggio Rusco. The landscape outside the window shifted slowly as we moved north — stacked hay bales, endless cornfields stretching to the horizon, a completely different energy from the dry, sun-bleached south I'd been living in for two weeks. I watched it all scroll past and felt something open up inside me.

I arrived just before six in the evening and walked straight to my accommodation — the Youth Hostel, the cheapest place in all of South Tyrol. I'd been lucky to book it just a month earlier. A Korean guy sharing my room told me he'd reserved his bed over a year in advance. That's when I realized what kind of place I was heading into.

After settling in, I walked the streets around the hostel to get my bearings — the supermarket, the bus stop, the small essentials of a new city. I found a bench on the street and sat down just in time to catch the last minutes of sunset. Those first moments in Northern Italy were pure and clean, like the air itself was telling me: something good is waiting ahead.

Day 2 — Tre Cime di Lavaredo: A Race Against the Clouds

Armed with a MobilCard, I set out on the long journey: three hours by train and bus from Bolzano through Brixen and Bruneck to Dobbiaco. I'd left at nine in the morning, but I still missed the only midday bus from Dobbiaco Station to the Tre Cime trailhead at noon. The next one wasn't until two in the afternoon. So I waited.

Those hours of waiting turned out to be a gift. I wandered the small town, drank water from a public fountain, and looked up at what might have been the clearest sky I've ever seen in my life. I felt like I'd stepped into a storybook.

The bus finally departed — and the rain arrived with it. The road up the mountain was a winding gray tunnel through heavy clouds. When I stepped off the bus at the top, the temperature had dropped to 9°C, the wind cut through my jacket, and the fog was so thick I could see maybe fifteen meters ahead. Cloud and mist swallowed the entrance. The Three Peaks were testing me before I'd even started walking.

It was already 15:30, which meant I had less than two hours to walk out and make it back for the last bus down at 17:00. So I moved fast. For an hour I pushed through the fog, unable to see much of anything — and then, suddenly, the clouds parted. The view that appeared behind them was worth every cold, wet minute. I stood there breathing it in, and for a moment the only word in my head was freedom.

I wish I'd had three hours to complete the full loop around the peaks. But maybe that's the universe's way of making sure I come back.

The train ride home to Bolzano stretched past ten at night. A long day — one of the longest — and one of the best.

Day 3 — Lago di Braies: The Emerald Valley

The same route again: Bolzano to Brixen to Bruneck to Dobbiaco, three hours of trains and buses that were starting to feel like a familiar commute. But the destination was new, and nothing — not the videos I'd watched, not the photos I'd studied — prepared me for seeing Lago di Braies in person.

The water was an emerald so vivid it didn't look real. A sudden rain shower passed through while I stood at the shore, and in the distance the mountains rose in pale limestone against the gray sky. I felt like I was sinking into some enchanted valley, a place designed to make you forget that the rest of the world exists.

I walked around the lake for over two hours. At some point, my mind drifted to a fantasy: wedding photos here. A white dress reflected in the jade-green water, the mountains rising behind. Right in the middle of the lake. A ridiculous, beautiful thought — and I let myself have it.

Day 4 — Moving to the Mountaintop

My last breakfast at the Youth Hostel. I took my time, studying the wall covered in notes and photos left by past guests — their messages, their memories, proof that they'd passed through this place and come out the other side happy.

Then I packed up and rode the bus for an hour, climbing to a new Airbnb perched high on a ridge above Bolzano. When I sat down for my first dinner there and looked out the window, the view explained everything. The valley spread below, the mountains framing it on all sides, the light turning gold. This was peace, served on a plate alongside whatever I was eating.

That night I sat with a map and planned the days ahead.

Day 5 — St. Magdalena: The Church in the Meadow

A gentle hike through green meadows so vivid they looked painted. I walked until I found myself sitting in the middle of a vast field, looking across at the small church of St. Magdalena. Its bell rang out at intervals — slow, unhurried, echoing across the grass. I sat there for a full hour doing nothing but watching and listening, eating snacks and the small local plums that are everywhere in South Tyrol. Some moments don't need to be anything more than what they are.

Day 6 — Alpe di Siusi: Lost in Scale

Lost in the beauty of Alpe di Siusi. Nature reminds me how small I am.

Two hours of walking through Europe's largest high-altitude meadow, seeing the same landscape from angle after angle, and each one more staggering than the last. The plateau stretches so far in every direction that your sense of distance breaks down. You're not hiking; you're floating through a painting that keeps getting bigger.

Day 7 — Seceda: Earning the View

I chose to walk up instead of taking the cable car. Two and a half hours to the summit. An hour and a half back down. My legs burned, my lungs ached, and I earned every single meter of it.

At the top, I sat in a meadow of wildflowers and looked out at the famous jagged ridgeline of Seceda — the serrated teeth of the Dolomites against a sky so blue it hurt. I'd seen this image on a thousand Instagram posts, but sitting there, wind in my face, heart still pounding from the climb — it was mine now. All of it was worth it.

Day 8 — Farewell to the Dolomites, Hello Lake Garda

Saying goodbye to the Dolomites was harder than I expected. This was a place where humans and nature exist in a kind of harmony I hadn't felt before — the pride of the Italian people, visible in every maintained trail, every clean valley, every mountain that looks like it was placed there by an artist. Even the views from the bus window on the way down were enough to make my heart ache.

I took the train to Verona, then a bus to an Airbnb near Lake Garda. That evening, I caught a local bus along the lakeshore, picked up groceries, and settled in. By coincidence, a couple at the guesthouse was celebrating a birthday, and they invited me to join. A cold beer, warm conversation, the lake glittering in the dark.

That same night, I met a British cyclist who was pedaling his way around Europe. He told me he spends three months every year cycling the world — that he wants to see every continent by bicycle before he dies. That kind of conviction stays with you. It made me want to keep traveling, keep moving, keep saying yes.

Day 9 — Riva del Garda and the Castle at Malcesine

An early bus to Riva del Garda, one of the most beautiful towns on the lake. The waterfront was the kind of thing I thought only existed in films — bright, impossibly blue, lined with cafés and boats. The mountains dropped straight into the water behind the town like a stage backdrop.

In the afternoon, I continued south to Malcesine and climbed to the top of the Scaligero Castle. Standing on the castle roof, looking out over the lake and the mountains beyond, I thought: this is a scene from a James Bond film. And I'm standing in it.

Day 10 — The Long Way Home

Up at six. A bus back to Verona, a train to Ferrara to collect a package of gifts from Donato for the team in Vietnam, then onward to Bologna Airport. The 14:55 flight lifted off for Hanoi, transiting through Istanbul.

On that plane, I let myself look back. Three weeks in Italy. The work in Avellino and Ferrara, and then this — the mountains, the lakes, the strangers who became friends for a day or an evening.

The People I Met Along the Way

Travel is landscapes, yes. But it's also faces. And the faces from this trip are what I carry closest.

At the Youth Hostel in Bolzano: the Japanese IT worker from Osaka, finally heading home after six years of saving money in Germany. The Dutch opera teacher, still searching for his breakthrough — I promised I'd become a fan. The Korean traveler who had planned his Dolomites trip over a year in advance; Italy was his twentieth country. Two teenagers who'd just graduated high school and cycled down from Austria for their summer holiday. And a man I met on the bus back to my mountaintop village, who told me he'd studied in America twenty years ago — I admired the arc of his life.

In Avellino: Maria, who took the time to show me around, to talk, to share her own story. She'd left a good career in the UK to come home to Italy, to be near her family, to give her young son the best life she could. I found that brave.

In Ferrara: Alice and Federica — two colleagues I'd worked with remotely for over two years. Meeting them in person for the first time felt like meeting old friends. There was no distance at all. They welcomed me warmly, helped me with everything, and made me feel like I belonged. I was deeply grateful.

Some trips are planned months in advance. Some are earned through years of quiet work. This one was both. The Dolomites didn't just show me beautiful mountains — they showed me a version of myself that says yes to the climb, even when the clouds are thick and the bus leaves in two hours.

Italy, thank you. I'll be back for that full loop around Tre Cime. I promise.
