The Work

I am a BIM infrastructure engineer. So basically, I make 3D model/drawing of bridges.

I work at Socotec Vietnam, in Hanoi. We're the offshoring arm for Socotec's European offices — Italy, mostly, but also Germany, France, Spain, the US, the UK. The work goes the other direction from what most people imagine: European engineers hand us problems, and we solve them from a small office a few hours north of the equator.

The Japanese Company

My first job was at a Japanese company called Chodai & Kiso - Jiban Vietnam, in Hanoi. There, I had the opportunity to work on major projects-from the Yên Xá wastewater treatment plant and the North-South expressway to various bridges across Vietnam, and notably, in Japan. Japanese projects demanded precision down to the millimeter, supported by highly accurate and well-synchronized input data-a stark contrast to what I had been used to in Vietnam. It was in that environment that I learned the value of honesty in work, the discipline, and the quiet diligence of the Japanese. Those qualities didn’t just shape how I work-they became principles I carry with me in who I am.

The day I became a manager

There came a point when the client asked to scale up the team as the workload grew. The pay increased slightly-but the responsibility doubled. It was no longer just about my own output; I had to care about the performance of others as well. I chose to see it as a challenge. The client specifically wanted more people with a similar capability to mine. That, in a way, validated my personal brand. The real test then became leadership: aligning everyone toward a shared goal, making sure we moved as one team. Along the way, I learned how to delegate effectively, empower others with ownership, and keep them motivated.

In reality, not everything went as expected. There were moments when team members weren’t fully honest, or drifted away from the plan. I believed that giving them an extra day in each project to rest or learn something new, would help. But it backfired. That flexibility was often used at the start, only for pressure to build as deadlines approached. The result? Rushed work, rising tension and I found myself growing frustrated, increasingly dissatisfied. At that point, the situation became much harder to manage than I had anticipated.

How I keep my team motivated

Keeping people motivated to grow is essential if you want them to stay. With that in mind, I created a monthly program called Internal Session—a space where the team connects, reflects, and moves forward together: - Team members stay aligned with company updates and direction through the team leader. - Everyone has a voice—sharing challenges, exchanging feedback, and working collectively toward better solutions. - We map out approaches for new types of projects, thinking ahead rather than reacting late. - And each session includes a technical sharing, where one member takes the stage—building confidence, being heard, and showcasing their expertise. Simple in structure, but powerful in keeping the team engaged and growing together.

How do I keep everyone aligned on a project?

Tell them where we’re going and how I would get there if I were doing the work. Then let them challenge it. If their approach makes sense, we go with it.

I make the purpose clear: what the inputs are, what the output should look like, how to execute, and how I estimate the timeline. Beyond that, I build “standards” - clear work instructions, common mistakes to avoid, what counts as an error, and how we evaluate quality internally.

Seeking for automation/innovation

My evenings, lately, have gone to a small ecosystem of plugins/tools I've been writing in dynamo with python or C#. They're small tools. Each one saves my team an hour or two a week. Collectively, they've given us back an afternoon every week - which is, in engineering terms, a very decent return.

What I care about at work

I care about incremental progress over grand gestures. I care about writing things down so the next person doesn't have to rediscover them. I care about my team enjoying their Thursdays. And I care, quietly, about the slow accumulation of small improvements - because I've come to believe that is how most good things actually get built.
