Journal · December 2025 – January 2026 · Taipei, Taiwan

Eight Days in Taipei: Welcoming 2026 Under Lanterns and Rain

Some trips are planned. Others are earned. This one felt like the second kind - a gift to myself for a year that asked more of me than I expected to give. I spent the final eight days of 2025 and the first days of 2026 in Taipei, and I came home with a suitcase of snacks, a camera full of thousands of photos, a notebook stamped at every MRT station I passed through, and a heart that felt a little lighter than when I left.

Here's how it went.

Day 1 — Arrival: Noi Bai to Taoyuan

Our flight lifted off from Noi Bai at 11:30 and landed at Taoyuan around 14:00. After clearing immigration, we rode the MRT into the city for the first time — a small thing, maybe, but riding a new subway in a new country always feels like the moment a trip actually begins.

By the time we checked in, it was already 17:00. We dropped our bags and walked straight out to Ximending, the neon-lit night market that would become our evening anchor for the rest of the week. That first night, I had a traditional Oolong milk tea — my first ever in the land that invented it. It was, genuinely, the best I've ever tasted.

Day 2 — Shifen's Lanterns and the Magic of Jiufen

Morning breakfast back in Ximending: a warm, traditional Taiwanese egg-and-bacon sandwich. A quick stop at the famous rainbow crosswalk for photos. Then we rushed to catch the MRT toward Ruifang, our gateway to the old mining towns in the hills.

We missed the last morning bus to Shifen by minutes. Sometimes travel is like that. So we wandered around Ruifang, ate slowly, and finally caught the first afternoon bus at 2 PM. The rain — a heavy, insistent rain that had been falling all morning — refused to let up. But Shifen didn't care. The old train-track street was packed with people writing wishes onto paper lanterns and releasing them into the gray sky. I watched them rise, one after another, and felt something shift.

From Ruifang we took another bus up into the mountains to Jiufen, and this is where the day became something I'll remember for a long time. The narrow alleyways, the red lanterns glowing through the mist and the rain, the steam rising off a hot sweet bean soup in my hands — it was like stepping into a painting. I ate a juicy pork bao, warmed up with the soup, and then climbed up to the famous A-Mei Tea House for a proper pot of tea. On the bus back to Taipei that night, I was quietly, completely happy.

And then, as if the day needed one more chapter — an earthquake. My first one in East Asia. For a moment I didn't know what to do. A Japanese traveler next to me, a guy named Keisuke, laughed gently and told me he was used to tremors this size. For me, though, it was unforgettable. We ended up talking for a while. Sometimes the best part of travel is the strangers you meet at exactly the right moment.

Day 3 — Bopiliao, Longshan Temple, and Taipei 101

We started slow. The Bopiliao Historic Block in the morning — red brick, faded signage, quiet courtyards that felt like the city's older, softer voice. From there, Longshan Temple, where the incense was thick and the crowd of worshippers was astonishing in its devotion. I stood back and watched for a long time. Faith, here, feels like a living thing.

Lunch was a milk tea pilgrimage: Xing Fu Tang in Ximending, the one with the brown-sugar pearls that cook on an open flame. Worth every minute of the queue.

In the afternoon, Taipei 101. I had seen it in photos a hundred times, but standing at its base and looking up, I finally understood why this building is the city's treasure. Completed in 2004, it was the tallest building in the world until 2010 — a record held for six years, and still the tallest thing in Taipei. I walked between viewpoints, looped back to see it from every angle, and eventually went inside as night fell. The lights came on, the tower turned into a column of glass and gold, and I understood why Taipeiers speak of it with such pride.

A note from the future: A few weeks after I came home, on January 25, 2026, Alex Honnold free-soloed Taipei 101 — the first person ever to climb it without ropes. I watched the footage and thought: I stood there. I stood right there, at the base of this thing. Visiting a place just weeks before it becomes the center of the world's attention gave that memory a strange, lucky weight.

Day 4 — Yehliu Geopark and Elephant Mountain

An early bus — nearly two hours — took us north to Yehliu Geopark, where the coastline is shaped into honeycombs and mushrooms and the famous Queen's Head rock. Wind, salt, stone. A morning well spent.

In the afternoon, we climbed Elephant Mountain (Xiangshan) for sunset. The stone steps up are steep, and by the top my legs were trembling, but the reward was the entire city laid out below us with Taipei 101 in the middle like a pin holding the whole view together. I passed other climbers on the way up and thought: imagine getting to walk these steps every day. What a life that would be.

That night, back at the hotel, my legs ruined, I found a handwritten letter waiting for me under the my bed. From Keisuke. A goodbye note. I read it twice and smiled for a long time.

Day 5 — New Year's Eve: Fireworks Over Tamsui, Countdown at Taipei 101

December 31, 2025. The last day of a long year.

We started at the Chiang Kai-Shek Memorial Hall — the scale of the architecture surprised me, the white marble gates almost absurdly grand. Inside, there was an exhibition by an elderly painter, his life's work devoted to horses, hung in anticipation of the Year of the Horse. We lost three hours in that hall without noticing.

By the time we were hungry, we were in the right neighborhood for Hang Zhou Xiao Long Bao, and the queue was more than an hour long. We joined it anyway. The soup dumplings were worth every minute.

We rested in the afternoon, then took the MRT out to Tamsui around 4 PM for the waterfront fireworks at 20:26. Fifteen minutes of fireworks over the water, in the rain. Absurd and beautiful. Then — the scramble. We joined the thick river of people flowing back toward Taipei 101 for the countdown show at midnight, the one that explodes off the tower itself. We made it in time. The tower lit up the sky, the whole city cheered, and I stood there thinking: I am ending this year in exactly the right place.

The ride back was shoulder-to-shoulder. I didn't mind at all.

Day 6 — A Quiet New Year's Day

January 1, 2026. I slept until 11. No plans. No alarms. Just slow food and slow streets.

A bowl of beef noodle soup so rich and deep I ate in silence. Then the National Taiwan Museum, free on New Year's Day — packed with families, children, locals, a few tourists like me. I wandered through the exhibits and bought a few souvenirs on the way out.

We tried for a Tamsui sunset, hoping to finally catch the sea without rain. The rain, which had followed us loyally for over a week, refused to grant it. In the evening, we wandered through The Red House, its Victorian-era interior unexpectedly warm and strange, and called it a night.

Day 7 — Taipei Zoo, and a Last Boba at Midnight

10°C and drizzling. The city felt close and quiet. We went to the Taipei Zoo, which was somehow packed despite the cold — hundreds of species, an entire morning of animals, and I forgot I was freezing.

That evening, Carrefour: bags of boba tea powder, pineapple cakes, beef noodle kits — gifts for everyone back home. And then, as midnight approached, I felt a small pull. I pulled on my jacket, ran back out to Ximending one more time, and drank a last Xing Fu Tang under the streetlights. A quiet goodbye to a place that had fed me so well.

Day 8 — Home

Check-out at 10 AM. The hotel staff waved us out like family. A quick MRT transfer at NTU Hospital station, the red line out to Taoyuan, the long security line, and then the 14:15 flight lifting off for Hanoi.

What I'm Bringing Home

I don't know how to fully describe what Taipei meant to me. This trip was a reward — for a year I fought through, for not giving up on myself when it would have been easier to. I went hoping to rest. I came back with more than that.

I'm bringing home a suitcase heavy with snacks and gifts; a camera with thousands of photos I haven't even started sorting; a little notebook full of station stamps, collected one MRT ride at a time; and a heart that felt scratched at the start of the trip and feels a little mended by the end — patched with lantern light, rain, strangers' kindness, and the quiet satisfaction of standing on a mountain at sunset watching a city turn gold.

Taipei, thank you. See you again.
